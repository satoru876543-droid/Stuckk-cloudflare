Algebra Homework Help: Solving Equations Step by Step

Algebra trips people up not because the math is hard, but because it's easy to lose track of which step comes next. Here's how to work through the most common problem types — and a tool to check your work when you're stuck.

1. Linear equations (solving for x)

A linear equation like 2x + 5 = 17 always follows the same pattern: isolate x by undoing operations in reverse order.

Worked example

2x + 5 = 17
Subtract 5 from both sides: 2x = 12
Divide both sides by 2: x = 6

The rule that trips people up: whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other. Skipping this is the #1 source of wrong answers.

2. Quadratic equations

For equations like x² + 5x + 6 = 0, you generally have three tools: factoring, completing the square, or the quadratic formula. Try factoring first — it's fastest when it works.

Worked example

x² + 5x + 6 = 0
Find two numbers that multiply to 6 and add to 5 → 2 and 3
Factor: (x + 2)(x + 3) = 0
So x = -2 or x = -3

3. Word problems

Most algebra word problems fail not at the math, but at translation — turning sentences into equations. The fix: underline the unknown, give it a letter, then rewrite each sentence as a math statement one at a time before solving anything.

4. Common mistakes to avoid

  • Forgetting to apply an operation to both sides of the equation
  • Sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign
  • Distributing incorrectly, e.g. 3(x + 4) becoming 3x + 4 instead of 3x + 12
  • Losing track of negative signs in quadratics

How to Solve Math Word Problems, Step by Step

Word problems feel harder than they are because the math is hidden inside a sentence. Once you have a repeatable process for pulling it out, most word problems become routine.

The 4-step method

1Identify the unknown. What is the question actually asking for? Give it a letter, like x.

2Translate each sentence. Go line by line and turn each piece of information into a math expression before combining anything.

3Build one equation. Combine your translated pieces into a single equation connecting what you know to what you want.

4Solve, then check. Solve the equation, then plug your answer back into the original sentence to make sure it actually makes sense.

Worked example

"A train leaves the station traveling at 60 mph. Two hours later, a second train leaves the same station traveling at 90 mph on the same track. How long until the second train catches the first?"

Working it through

Unknown: time (in hours) after the second train leaves, call it t.
First train's total time when caught: t + 2 hours, distance = 60(t + 2)
Second train's distance: 90t
They meet when distances are equal: 90t = 60(t + 2)
90t = 60t + 120 → 30t = 120 → t = 4 hours

Why students get stuck here

  • Trying to solve before fully translating the sentence — leads to guessing the equation
  • Mixing up units (minutes vs. hours, dollars vs. cents)
  • Not checking whether the final answer makes sense in context (e.g. negative time, negative people)
